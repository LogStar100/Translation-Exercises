ALIAS GRACE
by Margaret Atwood

Margaret Atwood is a famed Canadian writer whose works usually encompass themes like gender, identity, religion, and "power politics." This book in particular fictionalizes the 1843 murders of a husband and wife in Canada West; two of their servants were convicted and hanged. Note the usage of "faux Victorian" English – enough antiquated terms are used to set the scene, but not enough to distract the viewer. Keep this in mind during your translation.

That went on well enough for a time, but I began to be uneasy, as Mr. Haraghy attempted liberties in the back passage while I was carrying out the dishes from the dining room; and although I remembered Mary's advice about kicking between the legs, I thought it would not be right to kick my employer, and might also lead to dismissal without a reference. But then one night I heard him outside the door of my attic chamber; I recognized his wheezy cough. He was fumbling with the latch of the door. I always locked myself in at night, but I knew that lock or no lock, sooner or later he would find a way of getting in, with a ladder if nothing else, and I couldn't sleep easy for thinking of it; and I needed my sleep, as I was very tired from the day's work; and once you are found with a man in your room you are the guilty one, no matter how they get in. As Mary used to say, there are some of the masters who think you owe them service twenty-four hours a day, and should do the main work flat on your back.

went on Watch the tense here: since it refers to a discrete period of time in the past, you should use the preterite.

for durante is probably better than por here due to the implication of a long period of time; however, either could work well here depending on if that mood is invoked throughout the rest of the passage.

uneasy A number of possibilities: nerviosa, incómoda, desasosegada.

liberties This has a clear sexual connotation in English which must be rendered in Spanish. Perhaps the most exact equivalent is propasarse.

passage pasillo not pasaje (that refers to literature/voyages)

carrying out Difficult to render economically: retirar, recoger, quitar, or llevar are appropriate. traer cannot be used, as it always means "bring to the place where we are speaking," never "take to another place." cargar doesn't give the idea of direction, although salir cargada de platos might work.

although No subjunctive necessary: she did remember Mary's advice.

kicking dar una patada is certainly most common, but a construction with puntapié works well.

dismissal Probably better to use a verbal construction here.

heard This isn't French – entender is not the word!

wheezy Requires careful translation: resollante is perhaps the closest; asmático is accurate but unfortunately lacks the onomatopoeic quality of the English.

cough la tos, not *el tos.

fumbling Must be rendered by some paraphrase like manosear con torpeza.

latch pasador or pestillo.

locked myself in me encerraba rather than just cerraba la puerta.

ladder escalera (escalón is just a rung).

for por.

thinking A preposition is always followed by the infinitive in Spanish, never the gerund. "think of" is pensar en.

day's work Could be economically translated by mi jornada.

you This passage is fairly colloquial, so you can use tú: "si te descubren/sorpreden;" for that same reason it is inappropriate to use the ser passive.

you are Try to bring out the contrastive stress of the original by a change in word order: eres tú la culpable / la culpable eres tú.

no matter comoquiera que entren is most economical, but more long-winded ones give an opportunity to use este pejoratively.

twenty-four veinticuatro with an 'i.'

flat on your back boca arriba is the standard expression.
